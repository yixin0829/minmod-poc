Mineralogy Lithology and Structure Mina Grandé Sur The best mineralization in this area is found in the most northern part of the sector where a highly Zn-mineralized body is located in dolomitized limestones. These rocks have a sandy silt and clayey silt texture with abundant Fe- oxides. The minerals observed consist mainly of hydrozincite that fills cavities and fractures, and smithsonite which is disseminated in the rock. Another important feature of this mineralization is the presence of hydrozincite in fractures within fresh gray limestone. This occurrence is mainly observed at the end of the mineralized zones or on their margins. Areas south of this zone are typically mantos, generally <10 metres thick, The host lithology in this area is mainly the dolomitized limestone and silty clays. The footwall of the mineralized zone is well defined by a sharp change to massive gray mudstone-type limestone (micrite). The footwall shows no indications of zinc mineralization but it does contain calcite veining as well as patches of calcite. This is thought to represent the product of calcium depletion in the overlying dolomitized host rocks by acidic solutions created during the oxidation of sphalerite. Mina Grandé Centro Mineralogy: in this zone the zinc mineralization is mostly in silty clays that are in filling possible karst cavities and forming small zinc-bearing bodies that maintain a NW-SE direction. The main zinc mineral in this area is smithsonite which occurs in silt-clay bodies and hydrozincite that occurs as open space fillings in dolomitized limestones. This particular area is characterized by the presence of an uppermost zone of iron oxides (gossan) that overlies the zinc mineralization as well as small iron oxide-rich bodies that are interspersed with the siltstone, dolomitized clay and limestone. These iron-oxide bodies generally do not contain Zn mineralization. The mineralization is mainly found in silty clays and in small zones in dolomitized limestones. The presence of bodies of iron oxides (gossan) in this area is the result of the oxidation and dissolution of pyrite that originally existed in the limestone. The sulphuric acid generated during this process was a fundamental by- product that resulted in the dissolution of zinc sulphides, and to a lesser extent lead sulphides. These metals were carried short distances in solution that deposited zinc and lead-carbonate and other oxidized mineral species as a result of the buffering effects of the host limestones which were dolomitized in the process. Mina Grandé Norté Zinc mineralization in this area occurs in silty brown clays, as well as in strongly fractured and dolomitized limestone with continuously mineralized sections over many metres. The area may be unique in the presence of a porous gray limestone that may be part of the roof of a karst cavity system containing clay-silt material with slightly dolomitized gray limestone clasts and smithsonite mineralization as veins in fractures and as cavity fillings. Smithsonite also occurs as a fine grained replacement with interspersed grains of oxidized sandy-silt textured material and rock fragments. The main zinc-bearing section is underlain by less mineralized and dolomitized limestone that transitions downwards into slightly dolomitized gray limestone containing calcite veinlets and patches that show no evidence of zinc mineralization. The mineralization is mainly found in silty clays and intensely weathered dolomitized limestones. The main Zn mineral in this zone is smithsonite in silt-clay material and hydrozincite that occurs as fracture fillings and replacements of the dolomitized limestone host rocks.
